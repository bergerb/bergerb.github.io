I've seen quite a few different improvements in technology in my career as a .NET developer.  New frameworks (.NET CORE), new tools (Entity Framework), css technologies (bootstrap), CI/CD (jenkins), TDD (xunit/nunit), docker, angular, react, and all of these tools gave novel improvements in development time.  That all have felt incremental.  AI is in a class of its own!  It's much more than a tool -- it is your own assistant!

The speed at which AI is changing and improving is unlike anything I have seen in my career _so far_.  Personally, I feel behind, but I think that is true for a lot of developers in 2026.  It looks like it is only going to increase.

## Coding

AI does a good job at writing code -- in fact, I haven't written any new code in 2026.  Coding as we have known it is probably a solved problem, but I think there is still space to innovate and deliver great products.  That is the crux of the modern-day software developer.

**Write code faster.**  I use my virtual assistant to generate boilerplate, scaffold features, and handle repetitive patterns.  What used to take hours now takes minutes.  The velocity shift is dramatic.

**Use multiple models.**  Different models have different strengths.  I get feedback from more than one to help find bugs, catch edge cases, and control quality.  Treating them like a panel of reviewers rather than a single source of truth leads to better outcomes.

**Build agent teams.**  The next frontier isn't just one AI assistant -- it's coordinated agents working together on different parts of a problem.  I'm looking forward to building agent teams to deliver more cohesive software.  Pairing with AI feels like a natural continuum of running dev teams.

## The Manager Mindset

The developers who are thriving with AI aren't the ones who treat it like a search engine -- they're the ones who treat it like a team.  Having managed multiple dev teams over the years, I've found that the same skills that make a good engineering manager translate directly to working effectively with AI.

**Set clear expectations.**  A vague prompt gets vague output, just like a vague ticket gets vague code.  The more context you provide -- the goal, the constraints, the existing patterns in your codebase -- the better the result.  Think of it as writing a good user story before handing it to a developer.

**Review, don't rubber-stamp.**  Just as you wouldn't ship code without a review, you shouldn't ship AI-generated code without scrutiny.  AI can be confidently wrong.  It is your job to understand the output, ask follow-up questions, and push back when something doesn't look right.

**Iterate and give feedback.**  Good managers don't just accept the first draft -- they coach.  The same is true here.  If the output isn't quite right, tell the AI what's off and why.  The back-and-forth is where the quality is built.

**Delegate the right things.**  Senior developers know what to delegate and what to own.  Boilerplate, scaffolding, and repetitive patterns are great candidates for AI.  Architecture decisions, security-sensitive logic, and anything that requires deep domain knowledge still need your expertise at the wheel.

The mindset shift is real, but it isn't foreign -- especially if you've led teams before.  You're still responsible for the outcome.  AI just gives you a faster, tireless collaborator to work with.

## Beyond Coding

This is an area where AI is probably even more useful for development teams.  The reasoning and natural language capabilities of AI really shine once you move outside the editor.

**Project management integration.**  Integrating with tools like Azure DevOps, GitHub Projects, or Jira becomes part of your natural flow.  AI can help spin up well-written tickets, summarize work, and keep backlogs healthy -- tasks that used to steal time from actual development.

**Tester collaboration.**  AI can take raw feedback from testers and turn it into structured, actionable bug reports.  It can bridge the communication gap between testers and developers without the back-and-forth.

**Documentation and communication.**  Pull request descriptions, architecture notes, runbooks -- AI handles the writing so you can focus on the thinking.  Good documentation is no longer an afterthought when the cost to produce it drops to near zero.
